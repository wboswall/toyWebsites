A Tour of Cosmic Nebulae

Select an image to view an interactive slide show of cosmic nebulae.
All images courtesy of the NASA Image Gallery.

A nebula coming from the Latin word for "cloud" is an instellar cloud of dust, hydrogen gas, and plasma. Very few nebulae are visible with the naked eye. The largest and most visible from Earth is the Great Nebula of Orion which can be viewed just below the belt in the constellation Orion. The original classification of nebulae included several objects such as galaxies that do not fit the astronomical definition. In some older publications you might see the Andromeda Galaxy referred to as the Andromeda "Nebula".

Many nebulae are formed by the graviational attraction of gasses in a stellar medium or ISM. The mutual attraction of these gases can cause the formation of stars in the center of nebula. Indeed nebulae may be the birthplaces of all stars, including our own sun. Recent photographs from the Hubble Telescope have given us a rich view of these stellar nurseries and aided our understanding of the process of star birth.

Other types of nebulae are formed at the end of a star's life. Planetary nebulae

Nebulaes can also be formed from supernovas as large-mass stars loses their stellar matter in violent explotions. The most famous of these nebulae is the Crab Nebula in the constellation Taurus. The supernova was recorded by Chinese and Arab astronomers in 1054. It was not until 1731 that astronomer John Bevis observed the nebulae and it was not until the 20th century that astronomers were able to trace back the expanded gases of the Crab Nebula to the violent explosion observed almost 1000 years earlier. This discovery was a key in understanding the nature, origins, and ultimate destiny of stellar nebulae.
